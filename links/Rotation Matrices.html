Introduction

A rotation matrix, RR, describes the rotation of an object in 3-D space. It was introduced on the previous two pages covering deformation gradients and polar decompositions.

The rotation matrix is closely related to, though different from, coordinate system transformation matrices, QQ, discussed on this coordinate transformation page and on this transformation matrix page.

A transformation matrix describes the rotation of a coordinate system while an object remains fixed. In contrast, a rotation matrix describes the rotation of an object in a fixed coordinate system. The amazing fact, and often a confusing one, is that each matrix is the transpose of the other.

This entire page is essentially the transpose of the transformation matrix page. The two pages together border on being redundant. However, there are subtle differences between the two and they are stressed on this page.

Summary

The rotation matrix, RR, is used in the rotation of vectors and tensors while the coordinate system remains fixed. The vector or tensor is usually related to some object that is actually undergoing the rotation, and the vector and/or tensor is along for the ride.

The general rules for applying the rotation matrix are the same as for the coordinate transformation matrix.

Vectors   v′v′==R⋅vR⋅v
2nd Rank Tensors   σ′σ′==R⋅σ⋅RTR⋅σ⋅RT
4th Rank Tensors   C′C′==R⋅R⋅C⋅RT⋅RTR⋅R⋅C⋅RT⋅RT

and in tensor notation...

Vectors   v′ivi′==RijvjRijvj
2nd Rank Tensors   σ′mnσmn′==RmiRnjσijRmiRnjσij
4th Rank Tensors   C′mnopCmnop′==RmiRnjRokRplCijklRmiRnjRokRplCijkl


In two dimensions, RR is

R=[cosθ−sinθsinθcosθ]R=[cos⁡θ−sin⁡θsin⁡θcos⁡θ]
where θθ is the rotation angle. Note that RR is just the transpose of QQ, i.e., R=QTR=QT and Q=RTQ=RT.

Rotation Matrix Properties

Rotation matrices have several special properties that, while easily seen in this discussion of 2-D vectors, are equally applicable to 3-D applications as well. This list is useful for checking the accuracy of a rotation matrix if questions arise. While a matrix still could be wrong even if it passes all these checks, it is definitely wrong if it fails even one!

  • The determinant of RR equals one.

  • The inverse of RR is its transpose (this is discussed at the bottom of this page).

  • The dot product of any row or column with itself equals one.
    Ex: (cosθi+sinθj)⋅(cosθi+sinθj)=1(cos⁡θi+sin⁡θj)⋅(cos⁡θi+sin⁡θj)=1

  • The dot product of any row with any other row equals zero.
    Ex: (cosθi+sinθj)⋅(−sinθi+cosθj)=0(cos⁡θi+sin⁡θj)⋅(−sin⁡θi+cos⁡θj)=0

  • The dot product of any column with any other column equals zero.
    Ex: (cosθi−sinθj)⋅(sinθi+cosθj)=0(cos⁡θi−sin⁡θj)⋅(sin⁡θi+cos⁡θj)=0

Multiplication of Rotation Matrices

Recall from above that the dot product of any two different rows or columns of a rotation matrix is zero, while the dot product of any row or column with itself is one. This can be written in matrix and tensor notation as

R⋅RT=IandRikRjk=δijR⋅RT=IandRikRjk=δij
This shows that the transpose of a rotation matrix is also its inverse.

Rotating the Potato...

Recall that the vector on the potato was

v=2^i+9^jv=2i^+9j^
and the 50 counter-clockwise rotation of the coordinate system yielded

v′=8.18^i′+4.25^j′v′=8.18i^′+4.25j^′
In contrast, rotating the potato itself 50 counter clockwise gives

{v′xv′y}=[cos50∘−sin50∘sin50∘cos50∘]{29}⟶{−5.6097.317}{vx′vy′}=[cos⁡50∘−sin⁡50∘sin⁡50∘cos⁡50∘]{29}⟶{−5.6097.317}
So the rotated vector is

v′=−5.609^i+7.317^jv′=−5.609i^+7.317j^
Note that the apostrophe is on v′v′ to signify that it is somehow different from the reference vv vector (rotated in this case), but it is not on ii and jj because they are still in the reference coordinate system.

Comparing Rotations to Transformations

The previous example rotated the potato and vector 50 counter-clockwise. This example will instead rotate the coordinate system 50 clockwise, so θθ will be -50 this time.

{v′xv′y}=[cos(-50∘)sin(-50∘)−sin(-50∘)cos(-50∘)]{29}⟶{−5.6097.317}{vx′vy′}=[cos⁡(-50∘)sin⁡(-50∘)−sin⁡(-50∘)cos⁡(-50∘)]{29}⟶{−5.6097.317}
So the vector can be written as

v′=−5.609^i′+7.317^j′v′=−5.609i^′+7.317j^′
in the rotated coordinate system. The important point to be made here is that the numerical values of the rotated vector in the 1st example and the vector in the oppositely-rotated coordinate system of this example, are the same.

The general definition of RR, in 3-D, is

R=⎡⎢⎣cos(x′,x)cos(y′,x)cos(z′,x)cos(x′,y)cos(y′,y)cos(z′,y)cos(x′,z)cos(y′,z)cos(z′,z)⎤⎥⎦R=[cos⁡(x′,x)cos⁡(y′,x)cos⁡(z′,x)cos⁡(x′,y)cos⁡(y′,y)cos⁡(z′,y)cos⁡(x′,z)cos⁡(y′,z)cos⁡(z′,z)]
where (x′,x)(x′,x) represents the angle between the x′x′ and xx axes, (x′,y)(x′,y) is the angle between the x′x′ and yy axes, etc. Yes, this is indeed the transpose of QQ.

3-D Reduction to 2-D

So a rotation about the zz-axis means that cos(z′,z)=1cos⁡(z′,z)=1 because the angle between z′z′ and zz remains 0. Meanwhile, cos(x′,z)=cos(y′,z)=cos(z′,x)=cos(z′,y)=0cos⁡(x′,z)=cos⁡(y′,z)=cos⁡(z′,x)=cos⁡(z′,y)=0 because the angles between these axes remains 90.

The angle between y′y′ and xx is (90∘+θ)(90∘+θ), and cos(y′,x)=cos(90∘+θ)=−sinθcos⁡(y′,x)=cos⁡(90∘+θ)=−sin⁡θ.

Likewise, the angle between x′x′ and yy is (90∘−θ)(90∘−θ), and cos(x′,y)=cos(90∘−θ)=sinθcos⁡(x′,y)=cos⁡(90∘−θ)=sin⁡θ.

All of this leads to

R=⎡⎢⎣cosθ−sinθ0sinθcosθ0001⎤⎥⎦R=[cos⁡θ−sin⁡θ0sin⁡θcos⁡θ0001]
which reveals the 2-D rotation matrix within the 3-D matrix.

An alternative way of interpreting and generating the rotation matrix is as follows.

R=⎡⎢ ⎢ ⎢ ⎢ ⎢ ⎢ ⎢ ⎢ ⎢⎣(x-compof i′)(x-compof j′)(x-compof k′)(y-compof i′)(y-compof j′)(y-compof k′)(z-compof i′)(z-compof j′)(z-compof k′)⎤⎥ ⎥ ⎥ ⎥ ⎥ ⎥ ⎥ ⎥ ⎥⎦R=[(x-compof i′)(x-compof j′)(x-compof k′)(y-compof i′)(y-compof j′)(y-compof k′)(z-compof i′)(z-compof j′)(z-compof k′)]
where "x-comp of i′i′" means the x-component of the i′i′ unit vector. Pay close attention here to which terms have primes on them and which don't. Don't confuse this with "the x'-component" because "the x'-comp of i′i′" is simply 1. Yet another way to say this is, "the first component of the i′i′ unit vector in the non-primed reference x,y,zx,y,z coordinate system." Once again, this is just the transpose of the QQ matrix.

Successive Rotations - Roe Convention

The 3-D rotation matrix can be viewed as a series of three successive rotations about coordinate axes. There must be dozens of variations of this since any combination of axes can be chosen in any order to rotate about. One popular choice is the so-called Roe convention.

As shown in the figure, it consists of (i) a rotation through angle ψψ about the z-axis, then (ii) a rotation of angle θθ about the new y-axis (which has already rotated itself due to the first rotation about z), and finally, (iii) a second rotation of ϕϕ about the now-tilted z-axis.

The Roe convention is very popular despite one key challenge it contains. That is... if θ=0θ=0, then ψψ and ϕϕ become indistinguishable and only the sum of the two is what matters.



R=⎡⎢⎣cosψ−sinψ0sinψcosψ0001⎤⎥⎦⎡⎢⎣cosθ0sinθ010−sinθ0cosθ⎤⎥⎦⎡⎢⎣cosϕ−sinϕ0sinϕcosϕ0001⎤⎥⎦=⎡⎢⎣cosψcosθcosϕ−sinψsinϕ−cosψcosθsinϕ−sinψcosϕcosψsinθsinψcosθcosϕ+cosψsinϕ−sinψcosθsinϕ+cosψcosϕsinψsinθ−sinθcosϕsinθsinϕcosθ⎤⎥⎦R=[cos⁡ψ−sin⁡ψ0sin⁡ψcos⁡ψ0001][cos⁡θ0sin⁡θ010−sin⁡θ0cos⁡θ][cos⁡ϕ−sin⁡ϕ0sin⁡ϕcos⁡ϕ0001]=[cos⁡ψcos⁡θcos⁡ϕ−sin⁡ψsin⁡ϕ−cos⁡ψcos⁡θsin⁡ϕ−sin⁡ψcos⁡ϕcos⁡ψsin⁡θsin⁡ψcos⁡θcos⁡ϕ+cos⁡ψsin⁡ϕ−sin⁡ψcos⁡θsin⁡ϕ+cos⁡ψcos⁡ϕsin⁡ψsin⁡θ−sin⁡θcos⁡ϕsin⁡θsin⁡ϕcos⁡θ]
This time, the rotation matrices are indeed written in the same order: ψ,θ,ϕψ,θ,ϕ.

Going In Reverse - Determining Roe Angles from a Rotation Matrix

Suppose you have a rotation matrix populated with values as shown here and would like to know what Roe transformation angles were responsible for producing the matrix.

R=⎡⎢⎣R11R12R13R21R22R23R31R32R33⎤⎥⎦R=[R11R12R13R21R22R23R31R32R33]
The first and easiest step is to look at the R33R33 component. It is simply the cosine of θθ, so θ=Cos−1(R33)θ=Cos−1(R33).

The second step is to determine ψψ as follows

R23R13=sinψsinθcosψsinθ=sinψcosψ=tanψR23R13=sin⁡ψsin⁡θcos⁡ψsin⁡θ=sin⁡ψcos⁡ψ=tan⁡ψ So
ψ=Tan−1(R23R13)ψ=Tan−1(R23R13) The same process leads to
ϕ=Tan−1(R32−R31)ϕ=Tan−1(R32−R31) So in summary
ψ=Tan−1(R23R13)θ=Cos−1(R33)ϕ=Tan−1(R32−R31)ψ=Tan−1(R23R13)θ=Cos−1(R33)ϕ=Tan−1(R32−R31)
UNLESS!!!....... θθ proves to be very small! In this case, sinθsin⁡θ will be very small and therefore, so will R31,R32,R13R31,R32,R13, and R23R23 because all these terms contain sinθsin⁡θ. This can lead to major round-off errors in any such calculations.

Fortunately, the solution for this (θ→0θ→0) is to recall that ψψ and ϕϕ become indistinguishable from each other. This permits ϕϕ to be set to zero, and ψψ to be computed according to ψ=Sin−1(R21)ψ=Sin−1(R21).

Example: Roe Angles from Matrix

Let's start with the matrix in the previous example and work backwards.

R=⎡⎢⎣−0.3062−0.91860.25000.8839−0.17680.4330−0.35360.35360.8660⎤⎥⎦R=[−0.3062−0.91860.25000.8839−0.17680.4330−0.35360.35360.8660]

ψ=Tan−1(R23R13)=Tan−1(0.43300.2500)=60∘ψ=Tan−1(R23R13)=Tan−1(0.43300.2500)=60∘

Rotation About An Axis

Yet another way of specifying the rotation matrix is through a rotation axis vector, pp, and a rotation angle, αα, about the pp axis.

In this case, the rotation matrix is written as

R=cosαI+(1−cosα)p⊗p−sinαPR=cos⁡αI+(1−cos⁡α)p⊗p−sin⁡αP
with
P=⎡⎢⎣0p3−p2−p30p1p2−p10⎤⎥⎦P=[0p3−p2−p30p1p2−p10]
Writing the matrix out gives

R=⎡⎢ ⎢⎣cosα+(1−cosα)p21(1−cosα)p1p2−sinαp3(1−cosα)p1p3+sinαp2(1−cosα)p2p1+sinαp3cosα+(1−cosα)p22(1−cosα)p2p3−sinαp1(1−cosα)p3p1−sinαp2(1−cosα)p3p2+sinαp1cosα+(1−cosα)p23⎤⎥ ⎥⎦R=[cos⁡α+(1−cos⁡α)p12(1−cos⁡α)p1p2−sin⁡αp3(1−cos⁡α)p1p3+sin⁡αp2(1−cos⁡α)p2p1+sin⁡αp3cos⁡α+(1−cos⁡α)p22(1−cos⁡α)p2p3−sin⁡αp1(1−cos⁡α)p3p1−sin⁡αp2(1−cos⁡α)p3p2+sin⁡αp1cos⁡α+(1−cos⁡α)p32]
As usual, this can be written quite concisely in tensor notation

Rij=cosαδij+(1−cosα)pipj−sinαϵijkpkRij=cos⁡αδij+(1−cos⁡α)pipj−sin⁡αϵijkpk
Note the similarity to the tensor equation of QQ. The only difference is the + or - in front of sinαsin⁡α.

Qij=cosαδij+(1−cosα)pipj+sinαϵijkpkQij=cos⁡αδij+(1−cos⁡α)pipj+sin⁡αϵijkpk

It is very important to recognize that pp is a unit vector. Using any other length will lead to incorrect results.

Also, it's actually more common to use θθ or ϕϕ as the rotation angle instead of αα. But αα is being used here to minimize confusion with ψ,θ,ψ,θ, and ϕϕ used as the Roe convention angles.

It is easy to visualize the coordinate rotations in this method. For example, the 2-D case can be reproduced by noting that the rotation is about the z-axis, so the vector is p=(0,0,1)p=(0,0,1). This leads to

R=⎡⎢⎣cosα−sinα0sinαcosα0001⎤⎥⎦R=[cos⁡α−sin⁡α0sin⁡αcos⁡α0001]
As with the Roe angles, it is possible to back-out pp and αα given a rotation matrix, RR. The first step is to determine αα. This is done by taking the trace of the matrix.

α=Cos−1{12[tr(R)−1]}α=Cos−1{12[tr(R)−1]}
Once αα is determined, the components of pp are computed by

p1=R32−R232sinαp2=R13−R312sinαp3=R21−R122sinαp1=R32−R232sin⁡αp2=R13−R312sin⁡αp3=R21−R122sin⁡α
The three equations can be conveniently summarized in tensor notation as

pi=−ϵijkRjk2sinαpi=−ϵijkRjk2sin⁡α
Note that pp becomes undefined when α=0α=0. This means, "the axis you rotate about doesn't matter if you don't rotate in the first place."

Backing Out P and α

Determine the single rotation, pp and αα, that is equivalent to that produced by the Roe angles used earlier: ψ=60∘,θ=30∘,ψ=60∘,θ=30∘, and ϕ=45∘ϕ=45∘.

Recall that the Roe angles ψ=60∘,θ=30∘,ψ=60∘,θ=30∘, and ϕ=45∘ϕ=45∘ lead to

R=⎡⎢⎣−0.3062−0.91860.25000.8839−0.17680.4330−0.35360.35360.8660⎤⎥⎦R=[−0.3062−0.91860.25000.8839−0.17680.4330−0.35360.35360.8660]
Solving for αα gives

α=Cos−1{12[−0.3062−0.1768+0.8660−1]}=108∘α=Cos−1{12[−0.3062−0.1768+0.8660−1]}=108∘
Solving for pp gives

p1=0.3536−0.43302sin108∘=−0.0417p1=0.3536−0.43302sin⁡108∘=−0.0417
p2=0.2500−(−0.3536)2sin108∘=0.3173p2=0.2500−(−0.3536)2sin⁡108∘=0.3173
p3=0.8839−(−0.9186)2sin108∘=0.9475p3=0.8839−(−0.9186)2sin⁡108∘=0.9475
So the Roe angles ψ=60∘,θ=30∘,ψ=60∘,θ=30∘, and ϕ=45∘ϕ=45∘ are equivalent to a single rotation of 108∘108∘ about the axis given by p=(−0.0417p=(−0.0417, 0.31730.3173, 0.9475)0.9475). Both sets lead to the same rotation of an object.
The above example closes the loop on converting rotations from one system, Roe, to a second system, about an axis. In this case, and any other, the transformation matrix RR is the go-between permitting the conversion. The procedure is always to calculate the RR matrix in the first system and then back-out the angles in the second system from RR.

Inverses and Transposes

All rotation matrices possess a rather remarkable property - their transpose is their inverse. So it becomes trivial to compute the inverse of such a matrix.

RT=R−1soRT⋅R=IRT=R−1soRT⋅R=I

Dot Product Example

The above RR matrix is
